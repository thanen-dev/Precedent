◈ SITUATION ASSESSMENT

Cambodia is navigating a structural inflection point: the 2029 EBA deadline is now inside a three-year planning horizon, compressing the window for trade diversification and democratic governance signalling that could preserve EU market access for 700,000 garment workers. Macro stability remains intact under Pornmoniroth's fiscal discipline, but growth projections (5.0–5.2% for 2026) sit below the 6–7% corridor Cambodia requires to sustain its upper-middle-income trajectory by 2030. The dynastic transition from Hun Sen to Hun Manet is structurally complete but operationally unresolved — patronage networks inherited from the father are still the load-bearing architecture of the state, creating friction with the technocratic reform agenda being advanced simultaneously by Serey, Vandeth, Naron, and Chanthol.

◈ DOCTRINE WATCH

**1. Chea Serey's de-dollarisation doctrine is accelerating faster than political consensus supports.** The Bakong cross-border QR expansion (Japan Phase 1 2025, Singapore and India pipeline) is building transaction infrastructure that locks in riel-denominated pathways before the broader economy has shifted behaviour. Serey is front-running political will — a deliberate institutional strategy, but one that creates exposure if a confidence shock hits the riel before critical mass is achieved. Watch for NBC communication on riel deposit incentive structures this week as a leading indicator.

**2. Chanthol's "platform state" pitch is diverging from Vandeth's digital-sovereignty logic.** Chanthol's FDI promotion framework prioritises making Cambodia the lowest-friction manufacturing entry point regardless of investor origin — explicitly including Chinese capital. Vandeth's digital infrastructure doctrine emphasises sovereign control over data flows and technical standards, with ASEAN and Western technical partnerships as the preferred vectors. These are not yet in open conflict, but they point in different structural directions: one optimises for capital velocity, the other for strategic autonomy. The Digital Economy and Business Committee, which both men touch, is the institutional pressure point.

**3. Hun Manet's hybrid growth doctrine is being stress-tested by the US tariff environment.** Manet's offer to cut tariffs by 19 percentage points (executed through Chanthol) represented the clearest revealed preference in his tenure: Western market preservation ranks above short-term revenue and above Chinese preference. This is a significant doctrinal signal. The question is whether it reflects durable strategy or tactical crisis management. If US tariff negotiations produce no durable framework by Q3 2026, Manet faces pressure to revert to the China-maximalist posture that is Hun Sen's legacy default.

⚠ CONFLICT ALERT

**The single most dangerous internal contradiction is between Say Sam Al's extraction-permitting model at the Ministry of Environment and the trade diversification agenda being advanced by Nimul, Siphana, and Serey.** This is not merely an environmental-policy disagreement — it is a structural threat to EU EBA retention and to the ESG compliance requirements now embedded in European and American supply chain due diligence law. Nimul's WTO/EBA roadmap and Siphana's rules-based integration doctrine both require Cambodia to demonstrate credible governance improvement. Say Sam Al's ministry is simultaneously producing the most visible evidence of governance deficit — concession approvals that bypass EIA procedures, deforestation acceleration, and near-zero enforcement against connected land developers. The political logic is coherent: Say Sam Al is embedded in CPP patronage networks (son-in-law of Supreme Court Justice Dith Munty, brother of NBC Deputy Governor Say Samath) that Manet cannot afford to rupture. But the policy incoherence is now externally legible. EU trade reviewers and international ESG monitors are reading the same concession approvals that domestic reformers cannot challenge. This contradiction will not be resolved by technocratic coordination — it requires a political decision by Manet on whether patronage preservation or trade architecture takes priority, and he has so far refused to make it.

◈ HISTORICAL PARALLEL

**Strongest match: Sri Lanka 2010–2022 (82% match on Funan Techo Canal / infrastructure-led growth / Chinese infrastructure dependency signal).**

The parallel is structurally precise: Sri Lanka under Rajapaksa pursued a China-financed infrastructure programme (Hambantota Port, Mattala Airport, Colombo Port City) that was justified on development grounds, generated patronage revenues for the ruling family, and produced debt-service obligations that eventually overwhelmed fiscal capacity — culminating in the 2022 sovereign default. Cambodia's Funan Techo Canal is not yet at Hambantota scale, but the structural logic is identical: a prestige infrastructure project with Chinese financing, questionable commercial returns, significant environmental footprint, and deep entanglement with CPP patronage revenue streams.

What the Sri Lanka case predicts for Cambodia's trajectory: the danger is not immediate — it is medium-term and contingent. Sri Lanka had a 10–12 year window between the infrastructure commitment and the fiscal collapse. Cambodia's window is probably similar, but it is compressing. The critical variable in Sri Lanka was that the infrastructure debt coincided with a terms-of-trade shock and a revenue collapse (COVID for Sri Lanka; potential EBA loss and US tariff pressure for Cambodia). Pornmoniroth's fiscal discipline is the circuit breaker that Sri Lanka lacked — but it is one official, and his institutionalism is not yet embedded in budget law in a way that survives a political override. **The Sri Lanka case predicts that Cambodia's fiscal vulnerability will remain latent and manageable until an external shock removes the buffer — at which point the infrastructure-debt overhang becomes acute very quickly.**
