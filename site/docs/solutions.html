Chinese FDI dependency + debt exposure
Cambodia's Chinese FDI (60%+ of inflows) creates structural dependency. Funan Canal debt burden adds fiscal fragility.
Case Country / Period What worked Cambodia relevance
LKA_FISCAL_COLLAPSE_2010_2022Sri Lanka 2010-2022The Hambantota model — Chinese-financed infrastructure in exchange for political alignment — is structurally fragile when the host country has a narrow revenue base and high import dependency. The lesson for Cambodia is direct: Funan Techo Canal and similar infrastructure is funded on the assumption of continued growth. If the trade preference base (EBA/GSP) erodes while debt service rises, the fiscal arithmetic can deteriorate faster than the political system can respond.Sri Lanka's infrastructure debt trap: Chinese-funded Hambantota port → sovereign default
IDN_AFC_RECOVERY_1998Indonesia 1997–2003Banking sector restructuring post-crisis: decade of NPL reduction and capital adequacy improvement made Indonesia resilient to 2008 global crisis; democratic transition dismantled patronage networks that made financial system fragileCambodia’s banking system shows early warning signs of Indonesia-1997 structural vulnerabilities: CPP-connected networks dominate credit allocation; household debt has grown rapidly (flagged by analysts in 2024); Chinese USD-denominated project financing creates balance sheet exposure. If a Cambodia-specific shock (EBA loss + Chinese FDI pullback) triggers a credit deterioration, the patronage-banking nexus is the amplification mechanism. The IMF lesson is explicit: conditionality that requires dismantling political patronage networks cannot be externally imposed — it requires internal political change.
ARG_FISCAL_COLLAPSE_2001Argentina 1999–2005Abandoning an unsustainable peg decisively rather than defending it with austerity; building primary fiscal surplus post-default; using competitive exchange rate to drive export recovery; negotiating a deep debt haircut that restored debt sustainabilityCambodia is highly dollarised (USD is de facto currency), carries significant Chinese USD-denominated debt, and is pursuing de-dollarisation (Riel/Bakong) while simultaneously planning EBA-transition fiscal adjustments. If EBA loss triggers a revenue shortfall and Cambodia tightens fiscal policy into a slowdown — the Argentine procyclicality trap is the exact risk. The NBC’s de-dollarisation timeline (Riel to 30% of transactions by 2028) and MEF’s USD-denominated budget assumptions may not be simultaneously achievable — this is Cambodia’s version of the convertibility trap.
RISK
Garment sector labour standards pressure
International brands impose stricter labour and environmental standards. Non-compliance triggers order diversion to Vietnam/Bangladesh.
Case Country / Period What worked Cambodia relevance
BGD_RANA_PLAZA_2013Bangladesh 2013–2018Legally binding buyer agreements with independent verification and worker complaint mechanisms; minimum wage increase as floor-setting mechanism; public international pressure creating private enforcement that outperformed government regulationCambodia’s garment sector faces a comparable inflection: EBA graduation removes the EU’s preferential access, but EU buyers often require ILO compliance standards as a condition of continued sourcing. If EBA loss is accompanied by buyer reputational pressure on labour standards — the Rana Plaza pattern applies. Cambodia’s risk is the opposite of Bangladesh’s success scenario: if labour conditions do not improve alongside market access loss, buyers may shift to Bangladesh or Vietnam rather than stay. The GMAC (Garment Manufacturers Association in Cambodia) model of government-industry co-regulation needs a legal bindingness upgrade that it currently lacks.
MEX_NAFTA_LABOR_1994Mexico 1994–2004Trade agreement as FDI magnet: NAFTA’s investment protections and tariff elimination did attract substantial FDI into manufacturing; auto sector in particular developed genuine capabilities over timeCambodia’s garment sector is structurally analogous to Mexico’s maquiladora model: export-oriented, foreign-owned, low domestic value capture, imported inputs (Chinese fabrics), labour-cost competitive. The Mexico lesson is that this model has an expiry date — it lasts only as long as Cambodia is the cheapest option. When Vietnam or Myanmar undercuts Cambodia’s labour costs, Cambodia faces the same China-shock that Mexico faced in 2001. The 2029 transition window is Cambodia’s equivalent of Mexico’s need to move up the value chain before cost competition eliminated the maquiladora advantage — and Cambodia, like Mexico, has no domestic supply chain policy that would enable the transition.
BGD_GARMENT_2012Bangladesh 2005-2020Bangladesh demonstrates that sustained growth on a single export pillar is achievable for decades — but the longer it runs, the harder structural transformation becomes. The sector's political power grows with its economic share. For Cambodia, the RMG parallel is direct: EBA partial withdrawal in 2020 was the first stress test of a structure Bangladesh faces amplified.Bangladesh pre-Rana Plaza: export concentration trap — 80% garments, no diversification
